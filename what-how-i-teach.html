How I Teach

My teaching
philosophy.

Every method I use exists for one reason: to make AI genuinely understood, not just memorised.

"A student who builds a broken AI learns more than a student who watches a perfect one. I teach them to make mistakes on purpose."

— Teaching philosophy · AI Explorer Program

1

Unplugged First, Screens Second

Every concept is introduced without a device. Students use cards, role-play, and physical sorting before ever opening a browser. This builds intuition that technology alone cannot give.

Example: "Be the AI" — one student plays an algorithm that must guess a sorting rule from examples given by classmates. They feel the limits of their own model.
2

Train It, Break It, Fix It

Students don't just run demos — they train their own models with bad data on purpose, watch them fail, and figure out why. Failure is the curriculum.

Example: Train Teachable Machine with only 3 images → 80% inaccurate. Then with 30 → 95% accurate. Students see the difference, not just hear about it.
3

Guided Inquiry, Not Lectures

I ask more questions than I answer. "What do you think would happen if…?" is my most-used sentence. Students form hypotheses, test them, and present their findings to the class.

Example: Instead of explaining bias, I ask: "Train this classifier only on one gender's faces. What happens?" Students discover bias through data, not definition.
4

Ethics Is Not Optional

Every module — even the K–5 ones — ends with a question about fairness, privacy, or trust. AI literacy without ethics is just programming. I teach both simultaneously.

Example: K–5 students discuss: "How would you feel if an AI could know your secret?" High schoolers write a formal ethical analysis of face recognition in schools.
5

Every Student Builds Something Real

No module ends without a student artifact — a classifier, chatbot, poster, or essay. They take it home. They show their parents. That pride is irreplaceable.

Example: Grade 6 demo day — each pair presents their "spam sorter" AI to the class and explains one limitation they discovered during testing.
Inside My Classroom

What a 45-minute
lesson looks like.

Every class follows the same rhythm — unpredictable content, predictable structure.

🎯
0–5 min

The Hook

One surprising fact, demo, or question. "Did you know the Netflix algorithm decides what you watch next — but it's never actually seen your face?" Students are curious before they know they're learning.

🤸
5–15 min

Unplugged Activity

A hands-on, no-device exercise that builds the core intuition. Card sorting, role-play, or a group game. Everyone participates — there's no backseat in my class.

💻
15–32 min

Build & Experiment

Students apply the concept using a real tool — Teachable Machine, Scratch, or Python. They have a specific task: build X. But they're encouraged to go off-script and see what breaks.

💬
32–42 min

Share & Challenge

Two or three pairs share what they made. The class asks one question each. I ask: "What would happen if you changed X?" Students become critics of each other's models.

🌱
42–45 min

The Ethics Moment

One question. No right answer. "If your classifier makes a mistake and someone is wrongly identified — who is responsible?" They leave thinking. That's the point.

How I Assess

I grade what
matters.

No trick questions. No rote memorisation. Students are assessed on understanding, creativity, and ethical thinking.
